Mark develops analytical models of how thermal and electrical dynamics couple in lithium ion cells. He develops mathematical models, numerical software, and parameterisation routines to tie link the theory to experiments.

A cell generates heat when it is used, which causes temperature gradients to form.  Batteries are a chemical reaction in a box, so the hotter parts see higher reaction rates and higher current throughputs. This changes how a cell behaves, but complex models are required to describe these heterogeneous effects. Mark's research builds simple differential-equation models which allow him to describe heterogeneous behaviours, and unpick the dynamics that emerge when electrical and thermal problems become coupled. 

By better understanding heterogeneous behaviours, we can start to do something about them. The target is more accurate battery management systems, so that existing cells give a better performance. This includes leveraging knowledge of emergent cell behaviours to develop more accurate state estimators; and designing control strategies around the extrema of a cell, so that maximal performance can be gained without violating safety bounds.
